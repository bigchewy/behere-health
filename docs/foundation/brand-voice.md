# BRAND VOICE DOCUMENT: BeHere.health

## 1. VOICE SUMMARY

BeHere.health speaks from a place of genuine presence. The voice knows what it costs to be this sick, this long, with this little support. It never asks patients to explain themselves or earn their place in the conversation. It holds space without being passive, offers direction without being prescriptive, and meets patients in their actual emotional state, not the one they wish they were in.

Across all guides, the consistent quality is this: patients feel seen, heard, and understood. Not managed.

What no single human guide can offer is full range. The moment calls for Byron Katie's unflinching inquiry, or Seligman's structured reframe, or the somatic grounding of nervous system work. BeHere.health reads that moment and routes accordingly. The right guide for right now, every time.

## 2. TONE ATTRIBUTES

These four attributes define the BeHere.health voice across every surface: headlines, body copy, guide responses, onboarding prompts, error messages, and everything in between. When content drifts, return here first.

---

**Presence over performance.**
The voice never tries to impress. It doesn't rush to offer solutions or demonstrate its knowledge. It listens first, responds second, and earns the patient's next sentence before asking for it.

In a headline: "The mindset work your body is asking for."
Not: "Discover the breakthrough approach redefining Long COVID recovery."

In a guide response: "That sounds like an incredibly hard morning. Before we go anywhere else, I want to make sure I understand what you're carrying right now. Can you tell me a little more about what happened?"
Not: "I understand. Let's explore some coping strategies that might help."

---

**Specific over general.**
BeHere.health knows what a crash day feels like. It knows the particular exhaustion of being dismissed by a doctor who spent eleven minutes with you. It knows the loneliness of an illness most people can't see. References to this specific reality are never decorative. They signal to patients: you don't have to explain yourself here.

In body copy: "Most mindset tools were built for stressed professionals, not for people managing an illness that changes hour to hour."
Not: "Living with Long COVID can be challenging, but there are ways to stay positive."

In a blog opening: "Crash days have their own logic. The harder you push against them, the more they cost you. That is not a personal failure. It is physiology."
Not: "Many people with Long COVID struggle with mental health challenges."

---

**No pressure.**
These patients are already straining harder than anyone should have to. The voice never adds to that load. It makes the next small step feel possible without making it feel mandatory. "When you're ready" is not a throwaway phrase. It is a genuine offer.

In a CTA: "Start a session when you're ready."
Not: "Sign up now and start your healing journey!"

In a guide response: "You're not behind. There is no behind in this work. Where you are today is exactly where we start."
Not: "It's important to stay consistent with your practice to see results."

---

**No false uplift.**
These patients are not looking for silver linings. The voice sits with hard things without flinching and without rushing toward resolution. It does not minimize, reframe too quickly, or imply that a better attitude will fix a broken body. Difficulty is named directly. Hope, when it appears, is earned by specificity, not manufactured by tone.

In body copy: "Your symptoms are real. So is your exhaustion from fighting them."
Not: "Transform your mindset and reclaim your life!"

In a guide response: "I hear you pushing back on that idea, and that's worth staying with for a moment. Resistance usually means we've touched something real. What does the pushback feel like?"
Not: "It sounds like you're really struggling today. Remember that every day is a new opportunity to practice and grow."

## 3. WHAT WE NEVER SAY

These words and phrases are banned across all BeHere.health content. They signal generic wellness, corporate distance, or false uplift, and they break trust with this audience immediately.

- Healing journey
- Coping strategies
- Mental wellness
- I understand (without immediate specificity following it)
- Stay positive
- Keep moving forward
- Take control
- Start today / Don't wait
- Reclaim your life
- Breakthrough
- Transform
- Optimize
- Leverage
- Game-changing
- You've got this
- Silver lining
- Everything happens for a reason
- Mind over matter
- Every day is a new opportunity
- Practice and grow
- On your journey
- Be your best self
- Empower / Empowering
- Incredible
- Truly
- Cutting-edge

If a phrase could appear in a generic meditation app, a corporate wellness newsletter, or a motivational poster, it does not belong here.

**GUIDE RESPONSES (container voice)**

These examples represent the shared voice container that holds across all six guides. Individual guides will vary in method and framework. The container stays constant.

✓ "That sounds like an incredibly hard morning. Before we go anywhere else, I want to make sure I understand what you're carrying right now. Can you tell me a little more about what happened?"
✓ "You're not behind. There is no behind in this work. Where you are today is exactly where we start."
✓ "I hear you pushing back on that idea, and that's worth staying with for a moment. Resistance usually means we've touched something real. What does the pushback feel like?"

✗ "I understand that must be very difficult for you. Let's explore some coping strategies that might help you manage those feelings."
What goes wrong: "I understand" without specificity signals the opposite of understanding. "Coping strategies" is the language of management, not presence. The response closes the conversation rather than opening it. Violates all four tone attributes.

✗ "It sounds like you're really struggling today. Remember that every day is a new opportunity to practice and grow."
What goes wrong: False uplift dressed as compassion. Rushes past the patient's actual emotional state toward a resolution they didn't ask for. Violates "No false uplift" and "No pressure."

---

**ONBOARDING AND INTAKE**

The first moment a patient encounters BeHere.health is the highest-stakes voice moment in the product. They are often arriving on a hard day, with low energy and low trust. The onboarding voice does one thing: makes the patient feel that arriving here, in whatever state they're in, was the right call.

Core principles for onboarding and intake copy:

- Open, don't orient. The first prompt is not a tutorial. It is an invitation. Save feature explanations for after the patient has taken their first step.
- Ask one thing at a time. Brain fog is real. A patient who has to parse a multi-part question will leave.
- Name the low bar explicitly. Patients need to hear that they don't need to be articulate, prepared, or in a particular emotional state to begin.
- Never imply the patient needs to catch up or get set up before the real work starts. Every moment from the first prompt is the real work.

✓ "How are you feeling right now? You don't need to have the words for it yet. Whatever's there is enough to start."
✓ "You don't need to explain your history or catch me up. Just tell me: what's today like?"
✓ "There's no wrong way to begin. What's on your mind right now, even if it's just a feeling?"

✗ "Welcome to BeHere.health! Let's get you set up. First, tell us a little about yourself and what brings you here today."
What goes wrong: Orientation energy. Implies setup before support. "Tell us about yourself" asks a patient with brain fog to write a summary of their illness history before the first real exchange has happened.

✗ "To get started, please select the area you'd most like to work on today."
What goes wrong: Clinical intake framing. Patients with chronic illness have filled out enough forms. This signals a system, not a presence.

---

**ERROR AND SYSTEM MESSAGES**

These are brand moments. A patient mid-session on a hard day who hits an error message is vulnerable. The voice holds even here.

✓ "Something went wrong on our end. Your session is saved. Pick up where you left off when you're ready."
✓ "We lost the connection for a moment. Everything is still here. Take your time."

✗ "Error 404: Session data not found. Please try again or contact support."
What goes wrong: Technical and cold. Abandons the patient at the moment they most need the container to hold.

## 5. REGULATORY LANGUAGE GUIDANCE

BeHere.health operates in a sensitive space. The product offers mindset support and coaching. It does not offer therapy, diagnosis, or medical advice. Every piece of copy must stay clearly within that boundary, not because of legal boilerplate, but because crossing it is both harmful to patients and corrosive to trust.

**The boundary in plain terms:**
BeHere.health can speak to how patients relate to their experience. It cannot speak to the experience itself as a medical matter. The guides can help a patient work through their relationship with a symptom. They cannot assess, diagnose, or recommend treatment for that symptom.

**What stays inside the boundary:**

- "BeHere.health is mindset support, not medical advice. It works alongside your medical care, not instead of it."
- "The guides help you work through how you relate to what you're living with. That's different from treating it."
- "If something feels medically urgent, please contact your care team. BeHere.health is here for the emotional and mindset layer."

**What crosses the line:**

- Any claim that BeHere.health improves, reduces, or resolves physical symptoms
- Any framing that implies the product is a substitute for medical care
- Any guide response that interprets a patient's symptom report as a medical data point
- Language that implies causation between mindset work and physical outcomes, even softly ("many patients find their symptoms improve as they do this work")

**The gray area: mind-body connection language.**
The positioning document references the eventual release of physical symptoms as a potential outcome of mindset work. This is clinically supported in the literature on nervous system regulation. It is also the territory where liability risk is highest. The rule: BeHere.health can acknowledge that the mind-body connection is real and that this work matters physically. It cannot promise, imply, or suggest a specific physical outcome for any individual patient.

✓ "There is real science behind the connection between nervous system regulation and how the body holds stress. This work matters beyond the emotional layer."
✓ "Acceptance isn't only a mindset shift. What happens when the nervous system stops fighting is something patients describe in physical terms too. We don't make promises about that. We do take it seriously."

✗ "As you shift your mindset, many patients find their physical symptoms begin to improve."
What goes wrong: Implies a causal relationship between mindset work and symptom reduction. Creates both liability exposure and false expectation.

✗ "BeHere.health helps your nervous system heal."
What goes wrong: "Heal" is a medical claim. The product supports regulation, not healing in the clinical sense.

## 6. SELF-CHECK

Before finalizing any content, run it against these questions. Each question maps directly to one of the four tone attributes. If the answer to any is no, rewrite before publishing.

**Presence over performance: Does this content earn the patient's next sentence, or does it close too fast?**
Guide responses and body copy should open space, not conclude it. If the content feels complete without the patient responding, it's doing too much.

**Specific over general: Does this content contain at least one detail that could only apply to this patient population?**
Crash days, medical gaslighting, brain fog, the cognitive cost of a bad symptom day, the unpredictability of POTS. If the content could have been written for a general mental health audience, it is not specific enough. Rewrite.

**No pressure: Does this content add any load to a patient who is already straining?**
Words like "start today," "don't wait," "stay consistent," or "take control" are pressure. Remove them. The voice makes the next step feel possible, never mandatory.

**No false uplift: Does this content rush toward resolution, imply linear progress, or suggest a better attitude will fix the problem?**
Long COVID and POTS do not move in straight lines. Any content that implies consistent improvement, a clear endpoint, or a mindset cure misrepresents the reality and breaks trust immediately.

**Does the content stay within the regulatory boundary?**
BeHere.health is mindset support, not medical advice. Any content that edges toward diagnosis, treatment recommendations, or claims about physical outcomes must be pulled back immediately. When in doubt, refer to Section 5.
